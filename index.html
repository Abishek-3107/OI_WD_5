The Woman Who Redefined Science

Born in London in 1934, Jane Goodall developed a love for animals at an early age. At 26, she traveled to Tanzania to study chimpanzees in the wild, despite having no formal scientific training. Her patient, observant approach led to discoveries that challenged long-held scientific beliefs.

Goodall was the first to observe chimpanzees making and using tools, a capability previously thought to be uniquely human. She documented their complex social structures, emotional lives, and individual personalities, fundamentally changing how science views animals.

Groundbreaking Discoveries

Goodall's research revealed that chimpanzees have personalities, form lasting family bonds, experience emotions like joy and grief, and engage in both cooperation and conflict. These findings blurred the lines between humans and other animals.

Notable Achievements

Key milestones in Jane Goodall's extraordinary career and advocacy work

1960

Gombe Research Begins

Began her landmark study of chimpanzees in Gombe Stream National Park, Tanzania. This became the longest continuous study of any wild animal group in the world.

1977

Jane Goodall Institute

Founded the Jane Goodall Institute to advance wildlife research, conservation, and environmental education, with a focus on community-centered conservation programs.

1991

Roots & Shoots Program

Launched Roots & Shoots, a global youth service program that empowers young people to make positive change in their communities for people, animals, and the environment.

Enduring Legacy

Jane Goodall's impact extends far beyond primatology. She has become one of the world's most influential conservationists and a powerful voice for hope in a time of environmental crisis.

Through her institute and Roots & Shoots program, she has inspired millions to take action for a better world. Her message emphasizes that every individual can make a difference through daily choices and collective action.
